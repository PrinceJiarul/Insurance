1. Definition & Nature of Insurance

Definition: Insurance is a financial agreement between two parties â€” the insurer (insurance company) and the insured (individual or organization) â€” where the insurer promises to compensate for a specific loss, damage, illness, or death in return for a fixed premium.

Example: You pay a yearly premium to an insurance company for health insurance. If you are hospitalized, the company pays your medical bills as per the policy.

Nature:

  • Contractual agreement:
    Insurance is a legally binding contract, governed by the Indian Contract Act, 1872, and the Insurance Act, 1938.
  • Risk transfer mechanism:
    Insurance helps to transfer the financial burden of uncertain future events (like fire, theft, accident, or death) from the insured to the insurer.
  • Mutual cooperation:
    A large number of people pay premiums, forming a pool. The insurer uses this pool to compensate those who suffer a loss.
  • Payment for contingencies:
    Insurance pays only when a certain event (death, damage, illness) occurs. If the event doesn't happen, the insurer keeps the premium.
  • Not profit-making:
    It is not meant to make profit for the insured, only to cover the financial loss suffered.

3. Kinds of Insurance

Insurance can be broadly classified into two categories:

A. Life Insurance

This type of insurance covers the risk associated with the life of a human being. It provides financial protection to the family or nominee in case of the insured person's death. It also serves as a savings and investment tool in some cases.

  • Term Insurance: A pure life cover policy that provides a large sum assured at low premium for a fixed term. If the policyholder dies during the term, the sum is paid to the nominee. No maturity benefit is given if the insured survives.
  • Endowment Policy: This provides both risk coverage and savings. If the policyholder survives the term, a lump sum is paid. If they die during the term, the nominee gets the sum assured.
  • Whole Life Policy: Provides coverage for the entire life of the insured. The sum assured is paid only after the death of the policyholder.
  • Money Back Policy: Offers periodic payments during the policy term plus final maturity benefits, making it a mix of insurance and income.

B. General Insurance

This type of insurance covers risks other than life. It is usually short-term and compensates for actual loss incurred. General insurance is crucial for businesses, vehicles, health, travel, and property protection.

  • Fire Insurance: Provides compensation for damage caused by fire to property such as buildings, stock, furniture, etc.
  • Marine Insurance: Covers goods, cargo, and ships from the risk of loss or damage during sea transport. It has three types: cargo insurance, hull insurance, and freight insurance.
  • Motor Insurance: Covers damages or losses arising from vehicle accidents, theft, or third-party liabilities. It is mandatory in India to have third-party motor insurance.
  • Health Insurance: Offers coverage against medical expenses like hospitalization, surgeries, or doctor consultation. It may be individual or family floater based.
  • Travel Insurance: Covers risks during travel like trip cancellation, baggage loss, passport loss, or medical emergencies abroad.
Example: A person having a motor insurance policy gets reimbursement for repairs after a road accident.

4. Principles of Insurance (Core Concepts)

These principles ensure fairness and proper functioning of insurance:

  • Utmost Good Faith (Uberrima Fides):
    Both insurer and insured must disclose all relevant information truthfully.
    Failure = contract becomes void.
    Example: If someone hides their heart disease from the insurer, their claim can be denied later.
  • Insurable Interest:
    The insured must suffer financial loss if the insured item/person is lost/damaged.
    Without insurable interest, the contract is invalid.
    Example: You can insure your own house, not your neighborâ€™s.
  • Indemnity:
    Applies to general insurance.
    The insured is restored to the original financial position before the loss. No profit allowed.
    Example: If your insured TV worth â‚¹30,000 is damaged, the insurer pays â‚¹30,000 or the current market value â€” not more.
  • Contribution:
    If the same subject is insured with multiple insurers, all will share the loss proportionately.
    Example: Two fire policies of â‚¹1 lakh each. If loss is â‚¹50,000, both companies pay â‚¹25,000 each.
  • Subrogation:
    After paying the claim, the insurer gets the legal right to recover the loss from a third party responsible for the damage.
  • Proximate Cause:
    The immediate, effective cause of loss must be covered by the policy.
    Example: If fire is caused by lightning, and the policy excludes lightning, claim will be denied.
  • Loss Minimization:
    The insured must take all reasonable steps to reduce the loss.

7. Who are the Main Parties in an Insurance Contract?

An insurance contract involves the following key parties:

  • Insurer: The insurance company that provides financial protection or compensation for losses under the terms of the insurance policy. Example: LIC, ICICI Lombard.
  • Insured: The individual or entity who purchases the insurance policy and receives coverage. Example: A person buying health insurance or a business insuring its property.
  • Policyholder: The person or entity that owns the insurance policy. The policyholder may or may not be the insured. Example: A parent buying health insurance for their child.
  • Beneficiary: The person or entity entitled to receive the claim amount or benefits upon the occurrence of the insured event. Example: A nominee receiving life insurance payout after the death of the insured.
  • Agent/Broker: The intermediary who facilitates the insurance process by connecting the insurer with the potential insured, providing guidance, and earning a commission. Example: An insurance agent selling health policies.
